# **Case Study 2: ISS + V.I.T.A.L. Applied to a Push–Pull Structure**  
*A therapist works with a client experiencing ambivalence and relational oscillation.*

---

## **Client Snapshot**
**Client:** “Jordan,” 29, graduate student  
**Presenting Issue:** Chronic ambivalence in romantic relationships  
**Underlying Structure:** Push–pull dynamic between intimacy and autonomy  
**Therapeutic Goal:** Increase structural awareness, reduce oscillation, strengthen stable agency

---

# **Part 1 — ISS in Action**

## **1. ISS Entry Point**
Therapist begins:

> “What feels most alive or charged for you right now?”

**Client Response:**  
“I keep getting close to my partner, then suddenly I feel trapped and pull away. Then I miss them and go back. It’s exhausting.”

**Clinician Note:**  
The “alive” material is the oscillation — not the relationship content.

---

## **2. Surface the Structure**
Therapist:

> “If you look at this as a structure, what shape does it have?”

**Client:**  
“It’s like a rubber band. I move in, then snap back out.”

**Clinician Note:**  
Structure identified: **Push–Pull Dynamic**  
- Push = desire for closeness  
- Pull = fear of engulfment  
- Oscillation = repeated movement between poles

---

## **3. Identify Forces**
Therapist:

> “What forces are acting inside this push–pull?”

**Client Identifies:**  
- Desire for connection  
- Fear of losing autonomy  
- Internalized belief: “Closeness means obligation”  
- Past relationship trauma  
- Partner’s need for reassurance  
- Personal need for space

**Clinician Note:**  
Forces are polarized and reactive.

---

## **4. Locate Position**
Therapist:

> “Where are you inside this structure?”

**Client:**  
“I’m stuck in the middle. When I’m close, I panic. When I’m distant, I feel guilty.”

**Clinician Note:**  
Client is positioned **between poles**, experiencing tension from both sides.

---

## **5. Define Movement**
Therapist:

> “Not a solution — just movement. What would a shift look like?”

**Client:**  
“Maybe staying still for a moment. Not rushing toward or away.”

**Clinician Note:**  
Movement = **pause** within the oscillation, not choosing a pole.

---

# **Part 2 — Applying V.I.T.A.L.**

## **V — Viewpoint**
**Client Viewpoint:** First‑person immersed in oscillation  
**Shift:** Therapist invites third‑person observation:

> “If you watch the push–pull from outside, what do you see?”

**Client:**  
“It’s predictable. I react before I think.”

---

## **I — Identity**
Therapist:

> “Which identities are activated?”

**Client:**  
“The independent one. The loving partner. They fight each other.”

**Clinician Note:**  
Identity conflict between **autonomy identity** and **attachment identity**.

---

## **T — Tension**
**Tensions Identified:**  
- Internal: autonomy vs. closeness  
- Interpersonal: partner’s reassurance needs vs. client’s space needs  
- Structural: oscillation between poles  
- Temporal: past engulfment trauma influencing present behavior

---

## **A — Agency**
Therapist:

> “Where do you feel agency? Where does it collapse?”

**Client:**  
“I feel agency when I’m alone. When I’m close to someone, I lose it.”

**Clinician Note:**  
Agency collapses at the **moment of intimacy**.

---

## **L — Landscape**
Client maps the broader landscape:  
- Partner’s anxious attachment  
- Family history of enmeshment  
- Academic stress  
- Limited emotional regulation skills  
- Social pressure to “be a good partner”

**Clinician Note:**  
Landscape reveals relational and contextual pressures amplifying the push–pull.

---

# **Part 3 — Integration**

Therapist:

> “What do you see now that you couldn’t see at the beginning?”

**Client:**  
“That I’m not indecisive — I’m reacting to two parts of myself that want opposite things.”

---

## **Clinical Insight**
Therapist reflects:  
- The client’s ambivalence is structural, not emotional indecision  
- The push–pull dynamic is driven by identity conflict  
- Agency restoration must occur *within* the oscillation, not outside it  
- Movement should be micro‑scaled to avoid triggering panic or guilt

---

## **Practice Adjustment**
Therapist plans to:  
- Introduce “pause moments” to interrupt oscillation  
- Explore autonomy without withdrawal  
- Explore closeness without engulfment  
- Use ISS to track oscillation patterns  
- Use V.I.T.A.L. to map identity conflict and tension points

---

# **Part 4 — Training Notes for Clinicians**

### **Why this case is effective for training**
- Demonstrates ISS with a **push–pull** structure rather than a loop  
- Shows how identity conflict drives oscillation  
- Highlights how agency collapses at specific relational moments  
- Models how movement can be defined as *pause* rather than action  
- Shows V.I.T.A.L. clarifying the dimensional architecture of ambivalence

### **How to use this in training**
- Have clinicians map the push–pull visually  
- Ask them to identify the poles and forces  
- Have them run ISS prompts on their own ambivalence patterns  
- Compare their own identity tensions with the client’s  
- Discuss how viewpoint shifts reduce oscillation intensity
