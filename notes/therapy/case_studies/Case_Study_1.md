# **Case Study: Applying ISS + V.I.T.A.L. in Clinical Practice**  
*A therapist uses ISS + V.I.T.A.L. with a client struggling with role conflict and identity tension.*

---

## **Client Snapshot**
**Client:** “Mara,” 38, senior project manager  
**Presenting Issue:** Persistent exhaustion, irritability, and a sense of “performing” at work  
**Underlying Pattern:** Role conflict between competence identity and relational identity  
**Therapeutic Goal:** Increase structural clarity, reduce internal conflict, restore agency

---

# **Part 1 — ISS in Action**

## **1. ISS Entry Point**
The therapist begins with the ISS opening question:

> “What feels most alive or charged for you right now?”

**Client Response:**  
“I’m tired of being the person everyone relies on. I feel invisible unless I’m performing.”

**Clinician Note:**  
The “alive” material is not the exhaustion — it’s the *structure* around being relied on and performing.

---

## **2. Surface the Structure**
The therapist asks:

> “If you look at what you just said as a structure, what shape does it have?”

**Client Reflection:**  
“It feels like a loop. I perform, people praise me, then expect more. I keep doing it because I don’t want to disappoint anyone.”

**Clinician Note:**  
Loop structure identified:  
**Performance → Praise → Expectation → Obligation → Performance**

---

## **3. Identify Forces**
Therapist:

> “What forces are acting inside this loop?”

**Client Identifies:**  
- Fear of disappointing others  
- Desire to be valued  
- Internalized identity as “the competent one”  
- External pressure from workplace culture  
- Habit of over-functioning

**Clinician Note:**  
Multiple forces create a self-reinforcing cycle.

---

## **4. Locate Position**
Therapist:

> “Where are you inside this structure?”

**Client:**  
“I’m stuck between wanting to be seen and needing to be competent. I don’t know how to step out of it.”

**Clinician Note:**  
Client is positioned *between* identity poles: **Visibility vs. Competence**.

---

## **5. Define Movement**
Therapist:

> “Not a solution — just movement. What would a shift look like?”

**Client:**  
“Maybe saying no once. Or letting myself be average at something.”

**Clinician Note:**  
Movement = **micro‑permission** to disrupt the loop.

---

# **Part 2 — Applying V.I.T.A.L.**

Now the therapist transitions:

> “Let’s look at this through the V.I.T.A.L. dimensions.”

---

## **V — Viewpoint**
**Client Viewpoint:** First‑person immersed  
**Shift:** Therapist invites a meta‑view:

> “If you observe this loop from above, what do you see?”

**Client:**  
“It’s predictable. I’m not actually choosing — I’m reacting.”

---

## **I — Identity**
Therapist:

> “Which identities are activated?”

**Client:**  
“The competent one. The reliable one. The invisible one.”

**Clinician Note:**  
Identity conflict is central.

---

## **T — Tension**
**Tensions Identified:**  
- Internal: desire vs. fear  
- Interpersonal: expectations vs. boundaries  
- Structural: loop dynamics  
- Temporal: childhood pattern of earning approval

---

## **A — Agency**
Therapist:

> “Where do you feel agency? Where does it collapse?”

**Client:**  
“I feel agency before the request comes. Once someone asks, I lose it.”

**Clinician Note:**  
Agency collapses at the **moment of relational demand**.

---

## **L — Landscape**
Client maps the broader landscape:  
- Workplace culture rewards over-functioning  
- Family history of approval-seeking  
- Lack of peer support  
- High personal standards  
- Fear of relational rupture

**Clinician Note:**  
Landscape reveals systemic reinforcement of the loop.

---

# **Part 3 — Integration**

Therapist:

> “What do you see now that you couldn’t see at the beginning?”

**Client:**  
“That I’m not tired because of work. I’m tired because I’m stuck in a loop that keeps me performing a role I don’t want.”

---

## **Clinical Insight**
The therapist reflects (internally):  
- The client’s exhaustion is structural, not emotional.  
- The identity conflict is the engine of the loop.  
- Agency restoration must occur *before* the moment of demand.  
- Movement should be micro‑scaled to avoid triggering collapse.

---

## **Practice Adjustment**
Therapist plans to:  
- Work on pre‑demand agency strengthening  
- Introduce micro‑boundaries  
- Explore identity alternatives beyond competence  
- Use ISS to track loop disruptions session‑to‑session  
- Use V.I.T.A.L. to map shifts in viewpoint and tension

---

# **Part 4 — Training Notes for Clinicians**

### **Why this case is effective for training**
- Shows ISS surfacing structure without content over-focus  
- Demonstrates V.I.T.A.L. as a dimensional lens, not a checklist  
- Highlights identity tension as a structural driver  
- Models how movement is defined without premature solutioning  
- Shows how clinicians can track agency collapse points  
- Provides a clear example of loop dynamics in real clients

### **How to use this in training**
- Have clinicians map the loop visually  
- Ask them to identify alternative structures  
- Have them run the same ISS prompts on themselves  
- Compare their own identity tensions with the client’s  
- Discuss how viewpoint shifts change therapeutic leverage
